The Evolution of Geometric Thought

Geometry has evolved from the practical measurement of land in ancient times to the abstract study of spaces equipped with various structures. The modern perspective of geometry is heavily intertwined with algebra, topology, and analysis.

Geometry dates back to the ancient Euclidean geometry. This seems to satisfy the most prototypical idea of geometry among people. It provides some viewpoints in different aspects:
The classic Euclidean geometry was fundamentally established on $\mathbb{R}^n$. It involves notions like angles, lengths, which rely on the inner product. Afterwards, people realized that geometry could happen on spaces equipped with metrics or topology(not necessarily inner product). This leads to the viewpoint that geometry is a space with metrics/topology.
The morphisms between two spaces should preserve the metrics, and all operations can be packed into a group. In classical Euclidean geometry, the spaces (geometry entities) exist a priori, while groups or transformations depend on these spaces.

Klein's Erlangen Program (1872)

Felix Klein profoundly changed our understanding of geometry. Instead of focusing on figures, Klein proposed that geometry is the study of properties of a space that are invariant under a given group of transformations.

For instance, let $X$ be a space and $G$ a group acting on $X$.
- Euclidean geometry is associated with the group of rigid motions (isometries).
- Projective geometry is associated with the projective group $PGL(n+1, \mathbb{F})$.
- Topology is the study of invariants under the group of all homeomorphisms.

The idea embedded in the Erlangen program by Klein is a radical departure from tradition. It positions groups or transformations as foundational objects, treating spaces merely as a consequence of these groups.

Algebraic Geometry

Classic algebraic geometry is built on varieties (the geometric spaces), where the ring of functions is derived from the space.
However, the Grothendieck school brought a revolution to these concepts. A ring exists a priori, and consequently, a space (the spectrum) is built over it. The concept of spectrum itself is revolutionary. It not only has points, but also has irreducible components in the classic AG. The spectrum connects back to the given ring via schemes. The isomorphism implies their viewpoint that the ring of functions exists prior to the geometric space.

Up to now, comparing Klein's and Grothendieck's philosophies reveals a striking resemblance: They both 'betray' the traditional mindset by asserting that algebraic structures exist prior to geometric spaces, rather than being subordinate to them.

Furthermore, modern AG brings something new to Klein's program. For any given group $G$, one can obtain a space acted upon by $G$. It is natural to ask: Can $X$ be concretely associated back to $G$? More specifically, is there a structure sheaf analog in the context of groups -- a group-theoretic structure sheaf such that when evaluated on $X$, it yields the group $G$ itself?
The answer is affirmative. This question leads to the theory of Geometric Invariant Theory (GIT) and stacks.
