The Evolution of Geometric Thought

Geometry has evolved from the practical measurement of land in ancient times to the abstract study of spaces equipped with various structures. The modern perspective of geometry is heavily intertwined with algebra, topology, and analysis.

Geometry dates back to the ancient Euclidean geometry. This seems satisfies the most prototypical figure of geometry among people. It provides some viewpoints in different aspects:
The classic Euclidean geometry was established on $\mathbb{R}^n$ actually. It has notions like angles, lengths. These notions rely on the inner product on it. Afterwards, people realized that geometry could happen on spaces equipped with metrics or topology(not necessarily inner product). This leads to the viewpoint that geometry is a space with metrics/topology.
The morphisms between two spaces should preserves the metrics. All operations can be packed into a group. In Euclidean geometry, the spaces (geometry entities) exist ad hoc. Groups or transformations depends on the spaces.

Klein's Erlangen Program (1872)

Felix Klein profoundly changed our understanding of geometry. Instead of focusing on figures, Klein proposed that geometry is the study of properties of a space that are invariant under a given group of transformations.

For instance, let $X$ be a space and $G$ a group acting on $X$.
- Euclidean geometry is associated with the group of rigid motions (isometries).
- Projective geometry is associated with the projective group $PGL(n+1, \mathbb{F})$.
- Topology is the study of invariants under the group of all homeomorphisms.

The idea embedded in the Erlangen program by Klein is a 'trait' of tradition. This idea put groups or transformations as a pre-determinantal object and spaces as consequence of groups.

3. Algebraic Geometry

Classic algebraic geometry is built on varieties (space in the geometric side). A ring of functions relies on the space.
However, Grothendieck schools made a 'trait' or revolution of this concepts. A ring exists a priori. As a consequence, a space (geometry) is built over that ring (the spectrum). Even the concept spectrum is a revolutionary one. It not only has points, but also has irreducible components in the classic AG. The spectrum connects back to the given ring via schemes. The isomorphism implies their viewpoint that the ring of functions exists prior to the geometric space.

Up to now, a comparison between the Klein's idea and the Grothendieck's idea bears a lot resemblence: They both 'betrary' the traditional idea, thinking that an algebraic structure could exist independently and ahead of the spaces, not as a subordinate.

In addition, AG brings the Klein's program something new. Given a group $G$, one can get the space acted by $G$. It is natural to ask: Is $X$ able to be associated back to $G$ concretely, just like in AG? More concretely, is there a counterpart of a structure sheaf in the context of group $G$ -- a group-theoretic structure sheaf such that when this 'sheaf' is evaluated on $X$, it is the group $G$ itself?
The answer is affirmative. This question leads to the theory of Geometric Invariant Theory (GIT) and stacks.
